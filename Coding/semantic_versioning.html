TOPIC: Semantic Versioning

Behold, my contribution notes on Semantic Versioning.

The simplest example of semantic versioning I could find was regarding patch notes to the Steam game Shovel Knight. Shovel Knight is a throwback to old platformers in the style of Megaman, but with the benefit of being published online patches and updates are available free to fix what would in the past be game-breaking bugs that would've required copious betatesting to iron out.

The most current version available is 2.4. The only major version update thusfar has been the addition of an entirely new gameplay option to play as one of the rivals, Plague Knight, in their own unique campaign (version 2.0). From there, because of an excessive amount of interaction, timing, situations and options for input*, there have been multiple patches and expansions for exploitability and challenges added. Before the upgrade to 2.4 the version id was 2.2.1, showing that more additional features were added before the most recent release. In context this version number only shows how much they've added and responded to player feedback, and fixed any bugs or glitches in the process.
*(Shovel Knight's campaign allows for basic movement, jump,and two initial attacks with accessibility to 3 minor abilities, 8 alternative armors, and 12 special items, with Plague Knight having an augment-able arsenal of three parts to bombs with six options each, allowing for 216 unique combinations for bombs, with 12 special items)
